Why Are You Entering This Space?

Project 4: Protocol

Site

The site is the entrance to the ID space in N25. Although the space is physically open, it is mostly used by people who already have a reason to be there. Students pass through it naturally for class, studio work, or meetings, while visitors may enter out of curiosity or simply follow someone else.

Before: the entrance before the intervention.

Protocol

The protocol of this site is invisible. People who belong to the space rarely hesitate before entering. They already know why they are there.

For others, entering can be more uncertain. They may be curious, or they may simply be following another person. This difference is not written on a sign, but it appears in the small moment before passing through the door.

What I Wanted to Reveal

I wanted to reveal the difference between purposeful entry and automatic entry. Most people do not stop to think about why they are entering a space. The decision is made unconsciously.

By asking a simple question at the moment of entry, I wanted to bring that invisible decision-making process to the surface.

Why are you entering this space?

Gesture

I added a QR code near the entrance with an arrow pointing toward it. The arrow is not only a direction marker. It is a small trigger for curiosity. Why is the arrow pointing there? What happens if the QR code is scanned?

After scanning the QR code, visitors see a webpage that asks them why they are entering the space. They have only three seconds to answer.

The available answers are: Class, Studio Work, Meeting, Just curious, and Following someone.

If the visitor chooses Class, Studio Work, or Meeting, the page responds as if they already have a clear reason to be there. If they choose Just curious, Following someone, or fail to answer within three seconds, the page asks them to think once more before entering.

After: the QR code and arrow installed at the site.

Why QR Code?

A normal “keep off” sign delivers its message immediately. People read it, understand it, and either obey or ignore it. I did not want to make a sign that simply tells people what to do.

The QR code works differently. Before receiving the message, people first encounter the arrow and the code. This creates a small gap of curiosity. The message is not given automatically; the visitor has to choose to access it.

This extra step is important because the project is about interrupting an automatic action. Many people enter the space quickly and without much thought. The QR code slows that process down and creates a brief pause before entry.

In this way, the QR code is not just a delivery tool. It is part of the gesture. The arrow creates curiosity, the QR code creates participation, and the webpage creates reflection.

Impact

The intervention introduced a small pause into an otherwise automatic action. Some people could answer immediately because their reason was already clear. Others hesitated, ran out of time, or selected curiosity-based responses.

That hesitation became evidence of the protocol itself. The project does not prevent people from entering. Instead, it gives them a brief and playful moment to notice why they are entering.

Interaction: a visitor scanning or using the webpage at the site.

Reflection

The goal was not to decide who belongs. The goal was to make people notice the reason they enter.

The protocol already existed. The intervention simply made it visible.
